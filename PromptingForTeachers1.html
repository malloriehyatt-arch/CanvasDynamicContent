Section 1

What Are Prompts?

Prompts are the questions or instructions we type into AI tools. In general, clearer and more detailed prompts lead to more useful outputs. AI tools can help educators get a strong first draft, generate ideas, and save time, but the results still need professional judgment. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Key idea: Think of prompting like giving directions to a teaching assistant. The more clearly you explain the goal, audience, and task, the better the result. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

What Can AI Help Educators Do?

The article highlights three especially useful classroom applications for AI tools. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

1. Answer Questions

Ask for explanations of content, teaching ideas, demonstrations, or ways to check student understanding.

2. Develop Resources

Create lesson plans, quizzes, homework, reading materials, parent letters, reflection questions, and more.

3. Troubleshoot Challenges

Describe a classroom problem and ask for suggestions, adaptations, or alternative strategies.

Important: The guide notes that language models are usually stronger with words than with precise calculations, and that educators should sense-check outputs instead of accepting them automatically.

Section 3

6 Prompt-Writing Moves

These moves organize the article’s tactics into a shorter, repeatable toolkit for educators.

1. Be Clear

Use simple language and say exactly what you want the AI to do.

2. Add Details

Include the grade level, topic, classroom context, available resources, and goal.

3. Set a Role

Tell the AI who to act like, such as a primary teacher, instructional coach, or assessment designer.

4. Use Constraints

Specify the output type, number of questions, length, headings, or format you want.

5. Break Tasks Into Steps

For complex tasks, ask the AI to complete the work step by step.

6. Revise with Follow-Up Prompts

If the first result is not right, ask the AI to simplify, adapt, shorten, or rewrite it.

Sample stronger prompt:
Act as an elementary science teacher. Create a Grade 4 lesson plan on the water cycle for a classroom with only paper, pencils, and chart paper. Include one hands-on activity, three discussion questions, and a 3-question exit ticket.

Section 5

Use AI Carefully

AI tools can be helpful, but they have limits. The article emphasizes the importance of checking outputs carefully and protecting privacy. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Before You Use an AI Response

  • Check the answer instead of assuming it is correct.
  • Give local context, grade level, and curriculum details when needed.
  • Remember that some tools may not have current or local information.
  • Ask the AI to simplify language if the output is too complex.
  • Do not share confidential or personal student information.
Best practice: Use AI for drafting, brainstorming, and adapting materials, then review the output with your own professional judgment. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

✅ Before You Move On

Strong prompts are usually clear, specific, structured, and revised over time. Start with a good first prompt, then improve the response through follow-up questions.
