Reasoning

Chain-of-thought prompting

Breaks a complex task into logical steps so the model reasons through the problem.

Best for: Step-by-step reasoning and problem solving.

Example: “Explain how a bill becomes a law in clear sequential steps.”

Reasoning Paths

Tree-of-thought prompting

Explores multiple possible next steps and develops more than one reasoning path.

Best for: Complex questions with multiple valid directions.

Example: “Analyze climate change by exploring environmental, economic, and social effects.”

Explanation Check

Maieutic prompting

Builds and checks explanation trees, then removes inconsistent lines of reasoning.

Best for: Complex commonsense reasoning that needs explanation consistency.

Example: “Explain why the sky is blue, then break down each part of the explanation.”

Depth

Complexity-based prompting

Compares multiple chains of thought and favors longer, more developed reasoning paths.

Best for: Difficult problems where deeper reasoning may improve answer quality.

Example: Using several multi-step math rollouts, then selecting the strongest shared conclusion.

Facts First

Generated knowledge prompting

Prompts the model to generate relevant facts before producing the final response.

Best for: Essays, explanations, or tasks that benefit from background knowledge.

Example: “List key facts about deforestation, then write a paragraph explaining its effects.”

Sequencing

Least-to-most prompting

Lists smaller subproblems first, then solves them in order from simple to complex.

Best for: Tasks where later steps depend on earlier ones.

Example: “List the steps needed to solve 2x + 3 = 11, then solve each step in sequence.”

Revision

Self-refine prompting

Generates a response, critiques it, and revises it to improve the result.

Best for: Writing improvement, revision cycles, and response polishing.

Example: “Write a short essay, critique it for missing examples, and revise it.”

Guidance

Directional-stimulus prompting

Uses cues or keywords to steer the model toward the desired kind of response.

Best for: Guiding tone, vocabulary, imagery, or focus.

Example: “Write a love poem that includes the words ‘heart,’ ‘passion,’ and ‘eternal.’”
